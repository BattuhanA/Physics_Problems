# 🚗 Relative Uncertainty Problem

## ❓ Question
A car's speedometer has a **5% uncertainty**.  
If the speedometer reads **60 km/h**, what is the range of the car's actual speed?

---

# 📘 Understanding Relative Uncertainty

Relative uncertainty tells us how much the measured value might differ from the real value.  

A **5% uncertainty** means the actual speed could be **5% higher or lower** than the displayed speed.

So the speed shown on the speedometer is not perfectly exact ⚠️

---

# 🧮 Step 1: Calculate the Uncertainty Amount

The speed shown is:

\[
60 \text{ km/h}
\]

The uncertainty percentage is:

\[
5\%
\]

We calculate 5% of 60:

\[
\frac{5}{100} \times 60
\]

\[
= 0.05 \times 60
\]

\[
= 3 \text{ km/h}
\]

✅ So the uncertainty is:

\[
\pm 3 \text{ km/h}
\]

This means the real speed can be **3 km/h less** or **3 km/h more** than 60 km/h.

---

# 📉 Step 2: Find the Minimum Speed

Subtract the uncertainty from the measured value:

\[
60 - 3 = 57
\]

🚘 Minimum possible speed:

\[
57 \text{ km/h}
\]

---

# 📈 Step 3: Find the Maximum Speed

Add the uncertainty to the measured value:

\[
60 + 3 = 63
\]

🚘 Maximum possible speed:

\[
63 \text{ km/h}
\]

---

# ✅ Final Answer

The car's actual speed lies between:

\[
57 \text{ km/h} \leq v \leq 63 \text{ km/h}
\]

🎯 Therefore, the possible speed range is:

# 🚗 57 km/h to 63 km/h

---

# 💡 Quick Interpretation

Even though the speedometer shows **60 km/h**, the real speed may actually be:

- a little slower 🐢 → **57 km/h**
- exactly correct 🎯 → **60 km/h**
- a little faster 🏎️ → **63 km/h**

because of the **5% measurement uncertainty**.
